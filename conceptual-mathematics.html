1.1 Article II. Isomorphisms: Retractions, sections, idempotents, automorphisms

1.1.1 Exercise 1

prove that \(1_A\) is an isomorphism.

There is an inverse of \(A \xrightarrow{1_A} A\) – namely, \(A \xrightarrow{1_A} A\), since:

  • \(1_A \circ 1_A = 1_A\), by the Identity Laws.
  • Since \(1_A\) has an inverse, \(1_A\) is an isomorphism.

Show that if \(A \xrightarrow{f} B\) is an isomorphism, and \(B \xrightarrow{g} A\) is an inverse for \(f\), then \(g\) is also an isomorphism

If \(B \xrightarrow{g} A\) is an inverse for \(A \xrightarrow{f} B\), then the following must hold:

  • \(g \circ f = 1_A\)
  • \(f \circ g = 1_B\)
  • Since these two properties must hold, \(f\) must be an inverse for \(g\).
  • Since \(g\) has an inverse, \(g\) must be an isomorphism.

Prove that if \(A \xrightarrow{f} B\) and \(B \xrightarrow{k} C\) are isomorphisms, \(A \xrightarrow{k \circ f} C\) is also an isomorphism.

If \(f\) and \(k\) are isomorphisms, they must have inverses \(B \xrightarrow{g} A\) and \(C \xrightarrow{l} B\) with the following properties:

  • \(g \circ f = 1_A\)
  • \(f \circ g = 1_B\)
  • \(l \circ k = 1_B\)
  • \(k \circ l = 1_C\)

If \(k \circ f\) is an isomorphism, it should have an inverse \(h\), with the following properties:

  • \(h \circ k \circ f = 1_A\)
  • \(k \circ f \circ h = 1_C\)

A map with such a property is the composition of the inverses of \(f\) and \(k\), namely \(g \circ l\), since:

  • \(g \circ l \circ k \circ f\)
  • \(g \circ (l \circ k) \circ f\) (by associativity)
  • \(= g \circ 1_B \circ f\) (by substitution)
  • \(= g \circ (1_B \circ f)\) (by associativity)
  • \(= g \circ f\) (by the identity laws)
  • \(= 1_A\) (by subsitution)
  • Therefore: \(g \circ l \circ k \circ f = 1_A\)
  • \(k \circ f \circ g \circ l\)
  • \(= k \circ (f \circ g) \circ l\) (by associativity)
  • \(= k \circ 1_B \circ l\) (by subsitution)
  • \(= k \circ (1_B \circ l)\) (by associativity)
  • \(= k \circ l\) (by the identity laws)
  • \(= 1_C\) (by substitution)
  • Therefore: \(k \circ f \circ g \circ l = 1_C\)

1.1.2 Exercise 2

Prove that, if \(B \xrightarrow{g} A\) and \(B \xrightarrow{k} A\) are both inverses for \(A \xrightarrow{f} B\), then \(g = k\)

  • See https://math.stackexchange.com/questions/2105875/prove-that-that-the-inverse-to-a-map-is-unique.

If \(g\) and \(k\) are both inverses for \(f\), then the following must hold:

  1. \(g \circ f = 1_A\)
  2. \(k \circ f = 1_A\)
  3. \(f \circ g = 1_B\)
  4. \(f \circ k = 1_B\)

Therefore, the following holds:

\[f \circ g = f \circ k\]

And furthermore:

\[g \circ f \circ g = g \circ f \circ k\]

  1. \((g \circ f) \circ g = (g \circ f) \circ k\) (by associativity)
  2. \(1_A \circ g = 1_A \circ k\) (by subsitution)
  3. \(g = k\) (by the identity laws)

1.1.3 Exercise 3

If \(f\) has an inverse, then \(f\) satisfies the two cancellation laws:

  • If \(f \circ h = f \circ k\), then \(h = k\)
  • If \(h \circ f = k \circ f\), then \(h = k\)

The following putative cancellation law is not valid, even if \(f\) has an inverse:

  • If \(h \circ f = f \circ k\), then \(h = k\).

Proof of the first cancellation law

Premise: \(f \circ h = f \circ k\)

It follows that:

\[f^{-1} (f \circ h) = f^{-1} (f \circ k)\]

And now by associativity:

\[(f^{-1} \circ f) \circ h = (f^{-1} \circ f) \circ k\]

By the inverse laws:

\[1_A \circ f = 1_A \circ k\]

By the identity laws:

\[f = k\]

Proof of the second cancellation law

Premise: \(h \circ f = k \circ f\)

It follows that:

\[(h \circ f) \circ f^{-1} = (k \circ f) \circ f^{-1}\]

And now by associativity:

\[h \circ (f \circ f^{-1}) = k \circ (f \circ f^{-1})\]

By the inverse laws:

\[h \circ 1_B = k \circ 1_B\]

By the identity laws:

\[h = k\]

Proof that the third putative cancellation law doesn't hold

Prove that the following cancellation law is not correct, even if f has an inverse:

  • If \(h \circ f = f \circ k\), then \(h = k\)

All we need to do to prove that this implication is false, is to find one triplet of maps \(f,h,k\), s.t., \(f\) has an inverse, \(h \circ f = f \circ k\), but \(h \neq k\).

Take the following two membered set:

\[A = \set{a,b}\]

Let \(f,h,k\) all be endomaps of \(A\), such that:

  • \(f(a) = b\) and \(f(b) = a\)
  • \(h(a) = a\) and \(h(b) = a\)
  • \(k(a)= b\) and \(k(b) = b\)

First, note that \(f^{-1} = f\), since \(f \circ f = 1_A\)

Furthermore, the following holds:

\(h \circ f = f \circ k\)

Since:

  • \(h \circ f(a) = a\) and \(h \circ f(b) = a\)
  • \(f \circ k(a) = a\) and \(f \circ k(b) = a\)

But, \(h \neq k\)!

1.1.4 Exercise 4

For each of the following maps, decide whether it is invertible – if it is, find a 'formula' for the inverse map.

\[\mathbb{R} \xrightarrow{f} \mathbb{R}\]

\[f(x) = 3x + 7\]

Here is the inverse:

\[g(x) = (x - 7) / 3\]

\[\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0} \xrightarrow{g} \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}\]

\[g(x) = x^2\]

Here is the inverse:

\[h(x) = \sqrt{x}\]

\[\mathbb{R} \xrightarrow{h} \mathbb{R}\]

\[h(x) = x^2\]

This isn't invertible, since taking the square root of any real number can never return a negative integer, but the square of a negative integer is defined.

\[\mathbb{R} \xrightarrow{k} \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}\]

\[k(x) = x^2\]

Again, this isn't invertible, for the same reason as before.

\(\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0} \xrightarrow{l} \mathbb{R}_\geq 0\)

\(l(x) = 1 / (x + 1)\)

This is invertible, here is its inverse:

\[m(x/y) = (y/x) - 1\]

1.1.5 Exercise 5

\[A = \set{b,p,q,r,s}\]

\[B = \set{0,1}\]

\(A \xrightarrow{g} C\) maps \(b,p,q\) to \(0\), and \(r,s\) to \(1\).

How many maps are there with \(g \circ f = 1_{\set{0,1}}\)?

Let's assume that \(B \xrightarrow{f} A\)

There are three choices for \(0\) that \(g\) will map back to \(0\), and there are two choices for \(1\) that \(g\) will map back to \(1\), therefore, there must be \(3 \times 2 = 6\) possible maps \(f\) that satisfy the following:

\[g \circ f = 1_{\set{0,1}}\]

Choosing a particular such \(f\), how many maps \(g\) (including the given one) satisfy the same equation?

Let's assume that \(f(0) = b\) and \(f(1) = r\)

The question now reduces to - how many maps \(g\) are there that map \(b\) to \(0\) and \(r\) to \(1\)? Holding these two properties constant, we have two choices for each of \(p,q\) and \(s\), therefore there are \(2 \times 2 \times 2 = 8\) possible maps \(g\) which satisfy the equation:

\[g \circ f = 1_{\set{0,1}}\]

1.1.7 Definitions of retraction and section

If \(A \xrightarrow{f} B\):

  • A retraction for \(f\) is a map \(B \xrightarrow{r} A\), where \(r \circ f = 1_A\).
  • A section for \(f\) is a map \(B \xrightarrow{s} A\), where \(f \circ s = 1_B\)

1.1.8 Exercise 6

Proposition 1': If the map \(A \xrightarrow{f} B\) has a retraction, then for any map \(A \xrightarrow{g} T\), there is a map \(B \xrightarrow{t} T\) for which \(t \circ f = g\).

Proof of Proposition 1'

Premise: \(f\) has a retraction.

Therefore, there is a map \(B \xrightarrow{r} A\), with the property:

\[r \circ f = 1_A\]

We can construct a map with the appropriate domain and codomain by composing \(g\) with \(r\).

\[t = g \circ r\]

Does this in fact satisfy the required equation? yes.

\[g \circ r \circ f = g\]

\[g \circ 1_A = g\]

1.1.9 Exercise 7

Proposition 2': Suppose the map \(A \xrightarrow{f} B\) has a section. Then, for any set \(T\) and any pair \(B \xrightarrow{t_1} T, B \xrightarrow{t_2} T\) of maps from \(B\) to \(T\), if \(t_1 \circ f = t_2 \circ f\), then \(t_1 = t_2\).
