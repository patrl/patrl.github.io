3.1.1 Exercise 1

prove that \(1_A\) is an isomorphism.

There is an inverse of \(A \xrightarrow{1_A} A\) – namely, \(A \xrightarrow{1_A} A\), since:

  • \(1_A \circ 1_A = 1_A\), by the Identity Laws.
  • Since \(1_A\) has an inverse, \(1_A\) is an isomorphism.

Show that if \(A \xrightarrow{f} B\) is an isomorphism, and \(B \xrightarrow{g} A\) is an inverse for \(f\), then \(g\) is also an isomorphism

If \(B \xrightarrow{g} A\) is an inverse for \(A \xrightarrow{f} B\), then the following must hold:

  • \(g \circ f = 1_A\)
  • \(f \circ g = 1_B\)
  • Since these two properties must hold, \(f\) must be an inverse for \(g\).
  • Since \(g\) has an inverse, \(g\) must be an isomorphism.

Prove that if \(A \xrightarrow{f} B\) and \(B \xrightarrow{k} C\) are isomorphisms, \(A \xrightarrow{k \circ f} C\) is also an isomorphism.

If \(f\) and \(k\) are isomorphisms, they must have inverses \(B \xrightarrow{g} A\) and \(C \xrightarrow{l} B\) with the following properties:

  • \(g \circ f = 1_A\)
  • \(f \circ g = 1_B\)
  • \(l \circ k = 1_B\)
  • \(k \circ l = 1_C\)

If \(k \circ f\) is an isomorphism, it should have an inverse \(h\), with the following properties:

  • \(h \circ k \circ f = 1_A\)
  • \(k \circ f \circ h = 1_C\)

A map with such a property is the composition of the inverses of \(f\) and \(k\), namely \(g \circ l\), since:

  • \(g \circ l \circ k \circ f\)
  • \(g \circ (l \circ k) \circ f\) (by associativity)
  • \(= g \circ 1_B \circ f\) (by substitution)
  • \(= g \circ (1_B \circ f)\) (by associativity)
  • \(= g \circ f\) (by the identity laws)
  • \(= 1_A\) (by subsitution)
  • Therefore: \(g \circ l \circ k \circ f = 1_A\)
  • \(k \circ f \circ g \circ l\)
  • \(= k \circ (f \circ g) \circ l\) (by associativity)
  • \(= k \circ 1_B \circ l\) (by subsitution)
  • \(= k \circ (1_B \circ l)\) (by associativity)
  • \(= k \circ l\) (by the identity laws)
  • \(= 1_C\) (by substitution)
  • Therefore: \(k \circ f \circ g \circ l = 1_C\)

3.1.2 Exercise 2

Prove that, if \(B \xrightarrow{g} A\) and \(B \xrightarrow{k} A\) are both inverses for \(A \xrightarrow{f} B\), then \(g = k\)

  • See https://math.stackexchange.com/questions/2105875/prove-that-that-the-inverse-to-a-map-is-unique.

If \(g\) and \(k\) are both inverses for \(f\), then the following must hold:

  1. \(g \circ f = 1_A\)
  2. \(k \circ f = 1_A\)
  3. \(f \circ g = 1_B\)
  4. \(f \circ k = 1_B\)

Therefore, the following holds:

\[f \circ g = f \circ k\]

And furthermore:

\[g \circ f \circ g = g \circ f \circ k\]

  1. \((g \circ f) \circ g = (g \circ f) \circ k\) (by associativity)
  2. \(1_A \circ g = 1_A \circ k\) (by subsitution)
  3. \(g = k\) (by the identity laws)

3.1.3 Exercise 3

If \(f\) has an inverse, then \(f\) satisfies the two cancellation laws:

  • If \(f \circ h = f \circ k\), then \(h = k\)
  • If \(h \circ f = k \circ f\), then \(h = k\)

The following putative cancellation law is not valid, even if \(f\) has an inverse:

  • If \(h \circ f = f \circ k\), then \(h = k\).

Proof of the first cancellation law

Premise: \(f \circ h = f \circ k\)

It follows that:

\[f^{-1} (f \circ h) = f^{-1} (f \circ k)\]

And now by associativity:

\[(f^{-1} \circ f) \circ h = (f^{-1} \circ f) \circ k\]

By the inverse laws:

\[1_A \circ f = 1_A \circ k\]

By the identity laws:

\[f = k\]

Proof of the second cancellation law

Premise: \(h \circ f = k \circ f\)

It follows that:

\[(h \circ f) \circ f^{-1} = (k \circ f) \circ f^{-1}\]

And now by associativity:

\[h \circ (f \circ f^{-1}) = k \circ (f \circ f^{-1})\]

By the inverse laws:

\[h \circ 1_B = k \circ 1_B\]

By the identity laws:

\[h = k\]

Proof that the third putative cancellation law doesn't hold

Prove that the following cancellation law is not correct, even if f has an inverse:

  • If \(h \circ f = f \circ k\), then \(h = k\)

All we need to do to prove that this implication is false, is to find one triplet of maps \(f,h,k\), s.t., \(f\) has an inverse, \(h \circ f = f \circ k\), but \(h \neq k\).

Take the following two membered set:

\[A = \set{a,b}\]

Let \(f,h,k\) all be endomaps of \(A\), such that:

  • \(f(a) = b\) and \(f(b) = a\)
  • \(h(a) = a\) and \(h(b) = a\)
  • \(k(a)= b\) and \(k(b) = b\)

First, note that \(f^{-1} = f\), since \(f \circ f = 1_A\)

Furthermore, the following holds:

\(h \circ f = f \circ k\)

Since:

  • \(h \circ f(a) = a\) and \(h \circ f(b) = a\)
  • \(f \circ k(a) = a\) and \(f \circ k(b) = a\)

But, \(h \neq k\)!

3.1.4 Exercise 4

For each of the following maps, decide whether it is invertible – if it is, find a 'formula' for the inverse map.

\[\mathbb{R} \xrightarrow{f} \mathbb{R}\]

\[f(x) = 3x + 7\]

Here is the inverse:

\[g(x) = (x - 7) / 3\]

\[\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0} \xrightarrow{g} \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}\]

\[g(x) = x^2\]

Here is the inverse:

\[h(x) = \sqrt{x}\]

\[\mathbb{R} \xrightarrow{h} \mathbb{R}\]

\[h(x) = x^2\]

This isn't invertible, since taking the square root of any real number can never return a negative integer, but the square of a negative integer is defined.

\[\mathbb{R} \xrightarrow{k} \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}\]

\[k(x) = x^2\]

Again, this isn't invertible, for the same reason as before.

\(\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0} \xrightarrow{l} \mathbb{R}_\geq 0\)

\(l(x) = 1 / (x + 1)\)

This is invertible, here is its inverse:

\[m(x/y) = (y/x) - 1\]

3.1.5 Exercise 5

\[A = \set{b,p,q,r,s}\]

\[B = \set{0,1}\]

\(A \xrightarrow{g} C\) maps \(b,p,q\) to \(0\), and \(r,s\) to \(1\).

How many maps are there with \(g \circ f = 1_{\set{0,1}}\)?

Let's assume that \(B \xrightarrow{f} A\)

There are three choices for \(0\) that \(g\) will map back to \(0\), and there are two choices for \(1\) that \(g\) will map back to \(1\), therefore, there must be \(3 \times 2 = 6\) possible maps \(f\) that satisfy the following:

\[g \circ f = 1_{\set{0,1}}\]

Choosing a particular such \(f\), how many maps \(g\) (including the given one) satisfy the same equation?

Let's assume that \(f(0) = b\) and \(f(1) = r\)

The question now reduces to - how many maps \(g\) are there that map \(b\) to \(0\) and \(r\) to \(1\)? Holding these two properties constant, we have two choices for each of \(p,q\) and \(s\), therefore there are \(2 \times 2 \times 2 = 8\) possible maps \(g\) which satisfy the equation:

\[g \circ f = 1_{\set{0,1}}\]

3.1.7 Definitions of retraction and section

If \(A \xrightarrow{f} B\):

  • A retraction for \(f\) is a map \(B \xrightarrow{r} A\), where \(r \circ f = 1_A\).
  • A section for \(f\) is a map \(B \xrightarrow{s} A\), where \(f \circ s = 1_B\)

3.1.8 Exercise 6

Proposition 1': If the map \(A \xrightarrow{f} B\) has a retraction, then for any map \(A \xrightarrow{g} T\), there is a map \(B \xrightarrow{t} T\) for which \(t \circ f = g\).

Proof of Proposition 1'

Premise: \(f\) has a retraction.

Therefore, there is a map \(B \xrightarrow{r} A\), with the property:

\[r \circ f = 1_A\]

We can construct a map with the appropriate domain and codomain by composing \(g\) with \(r\).

\[t = g \circ r\]

Does this in fact satisfy the required equation? yes.

\[g \circ r \circ f = g\]

\[g \circ 1_A = g\]

3.1.9 Exercise 7

Proposition 2': Suppose the map \(A \xrightarrow{f} B\) has a section. Then, for any set \(T\) and any pair \(B \xrightarrow{t_1} T, B \xrightarrow{t_2} T\) of maps from \(B\) to \(T\), if \(t_1 \circ f = t_2 \circ f\), then \(t_1 = t_2\).

If \(f\) has a section, then there is a map \(B \xrightarrow{s} A\), such that:

\[f \circ s = 1_B\]

Since:

\[t_1 \circ f = t_2 \circ f\]

It should follow that:

\[t_1 \circ f \circ s = t_2 \circ f \circ s\]

By associativity (on both sides of the equation), we get:

\[t_1 \circ 1_B = t_2 \circ 1_B\]

And by the identity laws (on both sides of the equation), we get:

\[t_1 = t_2\]

3.1.10 Exercise 8

Prove that the composite of two maps, each having sections, itself has a section.

Let's take any two maps \(A \xrightarrow{f} B\), and \(B \xrightarrow{g} C\), and assume that both \(f\) and \(g\) have sections. This means that there are two maps \(B \xrightarrow{s_1} A\) and \(C \xrightarrow{s_2} B\) with the following properties:

\[f \circ s_1 = 1_B\] \[g \circ s_2 = 1_C\]

If \(A \xrightarrow{g \circ f} C\) has a section, there is a map \(C \xrightarrow{s_3} A\), such that:

\(g \circ f \circ s_3 = 1_C\)

If we compose \(s_1\) and \(s_2\) we get a map with suitable domain and codomain:

\[C \xrightarrow{s_1 \circ s_2} A\]

Does this satisfy the equation above? It does.

\[g \circ f \circ s_1 \circ s_2\]

By associativity and subtitution, since \(s_1\) is a section for \(f\).

\[g \circ 1_B \circ s_2\]

By associativity and the identity laws, we get:

\[g \circ s_2\]

Since \(s_2\) is a section for \(g\), we get:

\[1_C\]

3.1.11 Exercise 9

Suppose \(r\) is a retraction of \(f\) (equivalently, \(f\) is a section of \(r\)), and let \(e = f \circ r\).

  • Show that \(e\) is an idempotent.
  • Show that if \(f\) is an isomorphism, then \(e\) is the identity.

Proof that \(e\) is an idempotent

Suppose we have a map \(A \xrightarrow{f} B\), if \(B \xrightarrow{r} A\) is a retraction of \(f\) (and concomitantly, \(f\) is a section for \(r\)), it follows that:

\[r \circ f = 1_A\]

Let \(e = f \circ r\). If \(e\) is an idempotent, then \(e \circ e = e\). In this particular case:

\[f \circ r \circ f \circ r = f \circ r\]

Does this satisfy the equation? Yes. By associativity and subsitution, since \(r\) is a retraction of \(f\):

\[f \circ 1_A \circ r = f \circ r\]

By associativity and the identity laws:

\[f \circ r = f \circ r\]

Proof that if \(f\) is an isomorphism, then \(e = 1_B\)

If \(f\) is an isomorphism, then the inverse \(B \xrightarrow{f^{-1}} A\) is such that:

\[f^{-1} \circ f = 1_A\]

\[f \circ f^{-1} = 1_B\]

And furthermore, \(f\) obeys the following cancellation law:

  • If \(h \circ f = k \circ f\), then \(h = k\)

Given that \(f\) obeys the cancellation rules, we can surmise that since:

\[r \circ f = f^{-1} \circ f (= 1_A)\]

When \(f\) is an isomorphism, the retraction of \(f\) is the inverse of \(f\):

\[r = f^{-1}\]

Since \(e = f \circ r\), by subsitution:

\[e = f \circ f^{-1}\]

And again, by substitution:

\[e = 1_B\]

3.1.12 Exercise 10

If \(A \xrightarrow{f} B \xrightarrow{g} C\) are both isomorphisms, then \(g \circ f\) is an isomorphism too, and \((g \circ f)^{-1} = f^{-1} \circ g^{-1}\)

If \(f\) and \(g\) are both ismorphisms, there are unique inverses \(B \xrightarrow{f^{-1}} A\) and \(C \xrightarrow{g^{-1}} B\), such that:

\[f \circ f^{-1} = 1_B\] \[f^{-1} \circ f = 1_A\] \[g \circ g^{-1} = 1_C\] \[g^{-1} \circ g = 1_B\]

If \(g \circ f\) is an isomorphism, there should be an inverse \(h\), such that:

\[g \circ f \circ h = 1_C\]

\[h \circ g \circ f = 1_A\]

\(f^{-1} \circ g^{-1}\) is such an inverse, since if we take \(h = f^{-1} \circ g^{-1}\), we can prove the two equations above. Starting with the first:

\[g \circ f \circ f^{-1} \circ g^{-1}\]

By subsitution:

\(g \circ 1_B \circ g^{-1}\)

By the identity laws:

\[g \circ g^{-1}\]

By substitution:

\[1_C\]

And the second equation:

\[f^{-1} \circ g^{-1} \circ g \circ f\]

By substitution:

\[f^{-1} \circ 1_B \circ f\]

By the identity laws:

\[f^{-1} \circ f\]

By substitution:

\[1_A\]

3.1.13 Exercise 11

\[A = \set{Fatima, Omer, Alysia}\]

\[B = \set{coffee, tea, cocoa}\]

Find an example of an isomorphism \(A \xrightarrow{f} B\).

If \(C= \set{0,1}\), can you find any isomorphism \(A \to C\)?

An example of an isomorphism \(f\), is the \(f\) that maps \(Fatima\) to \(coffee\), \(Omer\) to \(tea\) and \(Alysia\) to \(cocoa\).

We know that \(f\) is an isomorphism, since it has an inverse \(f^{-1}\) which maps \(coffee\) to \(Fatima\), \(tea\) to \(omer\) and \(cocoa\) to \(Alysia\).

There is no isomorphism from \(A \to C\).

3.1.14 Exercise 12

How many isomorphisms from \(A \to B\), how many automorphisms? Why should the answers be less than 27?

First things first, the answers should both be less than 27, since there are only 27 possible maps from \(A\) to \(B\), since \(|B^A| = 27\) in this instance.

The number of isomorphisms from \(A\) to \(B\) is \(3 * 2 * 1\), i.e., \(|A|!\). This is because, e.g., when deciding first what to map \(Fatima\) to, I have three choices. Once this decision is made, I have 2 choices about what to map \(Omer\) to. Once this decision is made, I only have 1 choice about what to map \(Alysia\) to. If two distinct members of \(A\) get mapped to the same member of \(B\), the resulting map will not be an isomorphism.

There are also \(3!\) automorphisms of \(A\).
