1.2 1. Isomorphisms

1.2.1 Exercise 1

  1. Prove that \(1_A\) is an isomorphism.

    There is an inverse of \(A \xrightarrow{1_A} A\) – namely, \(A \xrightarrow{1_A} A\), since:

    • \(1_A \circ 1_A = 1_A\), by the Identity Laws.
    • Since \(1_A\) has an inverse, \(1_A\) is an isomorphism.
  2. Show that if \(A \xrightarrow{f} B\) is an isomorphism, and \(B \xrightarrow{g} A\) is an inverse for \(f\), then \(g\) is also an isomorphism

    If \(B \xrightarrow{g} A\) is an inverse for \(A \xrightarrow{f} B\), then the following must hold:

    • \(g \circ f = 1_A\)
    • \(f \circ g = 1_B\)
    • Since these two properties must hold, \(f\) must be an inverse for \(g\).
    • Since \(g\) has an inverse, \(g\) must be an isomorphism.
  3. Prove that if \(A \xrightarrow{f} B\) and \(B \xrightarrow{k} C\) are isomorphisms, \(A \xrightarrow{k \circ f} C\) is also an isomorphism.

    If \(f\) and \(k\) are isomorphisms, they must have inverses \(B \xrightarrow{g} A\) and \(C \xrightarrow{l} B\) with the following properties:

    • \(g \circ f = 1_A\)
    • \(f \circ g = 1_B\)
    • \(l \circ k = 1_B\)
    • \(k \circ l = 1_C\)

    If \(k \circ f\) is an isomorphism, it should have an inverse \(h\), with the following properties:

    • \(h \circ k \circ f = 1_A\)
    • \(k \circ f \circ h = 1_C\)

    A map with such a property is the composition of the inverses of \(f\) and \(k\), namely \(g \circ l\), since:

    • \(g \circ l \circ k \circ f\)
    • \(g \circ (l \circ k) \circ f\) (by associativity)
    • \(= g \circ 1_B \circ f\) (by substitution)
    • \(= g \circ (1_B \circ f)\) (by associativity)
    • \(= g \circ f\) (by the identity laws)
    • \(= 1_A\) (by subsitution)
    • Therefore: \(g \circ l \circ k \circ f = 1_A\)
    • \(k \circ f \circ g \circ l\)
    • \(= k \circ (f \circ g) \circ l\) (by associativity)
    • \(= k \circ 1_B \circ l\) (by subsitution)
    • \(= k \circ (1_B \circ l)\) (by associativity)
    • \(= k \circ l\) (by the identity laws)
    • \(= 1_C\) (by substitution)
    • Therefore: \(k \circ f \circ g \circ l = 1_C\)

1.2.2 Exercise 2

  • Prove that, if \(B \xrightarrow{g} A\) and \(B \xrightarrow{k} A\) are both inverses for \(A \xrightarrow{f} B\), then \(g = k\)
  • See https://math.stackexchange.com/questions/2105875/prove-that-that-the-inverse-to-a-map-is-unique.

If \(g\) and \(k\) are both inverses for \(f\), then the following must hold:

  1. \(g \circ f = 1_A\)
  2. \(k \circ f = 1_A\)
  3. \(f \circ g = 1_B\)
  4. \(f \circ k = 1_B\)

Therefore, the following holds:

\[f \circ g = f \circ k\]

And furthermore:

\[g \circ f \circ g = g \circ f \circ k\]

  1. \((g \circ f) \circ g = (g \circ f) \circ k\) (by associativity)
  2. \(1_A \circ g = 1_A \circ k\) (by subsitution)
  3. \(g = k\) (by the identity laws)

1.2.3 Exercise 3

If \(f\) has an inverse, then \(f\) satisfies the two cancellation laws:

a. If \(f \circ h = f \circ k\), then \(h = k\) b. If \(h \circ f = k \circ f\), then \(h = k\)

The following putative cancellation law is not valid, even if \(f\) has an inverse:

c. If \(h \circ f = f \circ k\), then \(h = k\).

  1. Part (a)

    Premise: \(f \circ h = f \circ k\)

    It follows that:

    \[f^{-1} (f \circ h) = f^{-1} (f \circ k)\]

    And now by associativity:

    \[(f^{-1} \circ f) \circ h = (f^{-1} \circ f) \circ k\]

    By the inverse laws:

    \[1_A \circ f = 1_A \circ k\]

    By the identity laws:

    \[f = k\]

2.1 1.2

\(SPACE = PLANE × LINE\)

shadow is a map from space to the plane. We can think of it as a function from 3-dimensional coordinates to 2-dimensional coordinates. Differences in the z-axis essentially get flattened out.

level is a map from space to the line. We can think of it as a function from 3-dimensional coordinates to 1-dimensional coordinates. level takes the value of the z-axis and ignores the x and y axes.

Motion in space is reduced to motion in the plane and motion on the line.

The flight of bird is a function from times to points in space (3-dimensional coordinats).

Once we compose the flight of bird function with the level function and the shadow function, space completely disappears from the picture.

We can think of conjunction reduction as a complex operation decomposable into two functions.

3 Part 1

  • Categories have objects, and maps.
  • maps have domains and codomains.
  • A map in which the domain and codomain are identical is an endomap.
  • For every object in the category of finite sets, there is an identity map. The identity map of the finite set \(A\) is written \(1_A\)
  • external vs internal diagrams for maps in the category of finite sets.
  • The composition of maps is function composition.

3.0.1 Category definition

  • Objects: \(A, B, C, …\)
  • Maps: \(A \xrightarrow{f} B, …\)
  • Identity maps (one per object): \(A \xrightarrow{1_A} B, … \)
  • Composition of maps: assigns to each pair of maps of type \(A \xrightarrow{g} B \xrightarrow{f} C\) another map called "\(f\) following \(g\)", \(A \xrightarrow{f ∘ g} C\). Note, the codomain of \(g\) must be identical to the domain of \(f\).
  • Rules for a category:
    • The identity laws:
      1. If \(A \xrightarrow{1_A} A \xrightarrow{g} B\) then \(A \xrightarrow{g ∘ 1_A = g} B\)
      2. If \(A \xrightarrow{f} B\ \xrightarrow{1_B} B\) then \(A \xrightarrow{1_B ∘ f = f} B\)

    • The associative law:

      If \(A \xrightarrow{f} B \xrightarrow{g} C \xrightarrow{h} D\) then \(A \xrightarrow{h ∘ (g ∘ f)= (h ∘ g) ∘ f} D\)
