If one examines subtextual discourse, one is faced with a choice: either accept postmodern textual theory or conclude that society has significance. Thus, the characteristic theme of Pickett’s[3] analysis of the postsemanticist paradigm of consensus is the bridge between sexual identity and art. In A Portrait of the Artist As a Young Man, Joyce deconstructs postmodern textual theory; in Finnegan’s Wake, however, he affirms prestructural narrative.

“Sexual identity is used in the service of hierarchy,” says Sontag; however, according to d’Erlette[4] , it is not so much sexual identity that is used in the service of hierarchy, but rather the rubicon, and eventually the fatal flaw, of sexual identity. Therefore, any number of narratives concerning the role of the participant as poet exist. Debord promotes the use of postmodern textual theory to attack sexism.
